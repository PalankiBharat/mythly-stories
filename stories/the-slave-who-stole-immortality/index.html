Mahabharata · Adi Parva · Astika Parva

The Slave Who Stole Immortality from Heaven — Why Serpents Have Forked Tongues

Garuda’s Origin Adi Parva, Chapters 14–34 A Mother’s Freedom
· · ·

Have you ever looked closely at a snake’s tongue? It is split right down the middle — forked into two. The Mahabharata says it knows exactly why. And the reason goes back to a stolen pot of immortality, a rigged bet, and a son who fought every god in heaven to free his enslaved mother.

The sage Kashyapa had two wives — Kadru and Vinata. Both wanted children. According to the Adi Parva, Kashyapa granted each a boon. Kadru asked for a thousand serpent sons. Vinata asked for just two sons — but she wanted them to be more powerful than all of Kadru’s children combined.

In time, Kadru’s eggs hatched and produced a thousand serpents — the Nagas. But Vinata’s two eggs took much longer. Impatient, Vinata broke open one egg too early. Inside was Aruna, who was only half-formed. According to the text, Aruna cursed his mother for her impatience, saying she would become a slave. He rose into the sky and became the charioteer of Surya, the sun god. The second egg Vinata left alone. From it, in time, came Garuda.

But before Garuda was born, the damage was already done.

One day, Kadru and Vinata saw the divine horse Uchchaihshravas — the white horse that had appeared during the churning of the ocean. Kadru asked Vinata what color the horse’s tail was. Vinata said white. Kadru said it was black. They made a bet: whoever was wrong would become the other’s slave.

The horse’s tail was white. But Kadru cheated. She ordered her serpent sons to hang from the horse’s tail so it would look black. Many of her sons refused — and Kadru cursed those who refused, saying they would die in a snake sacrifice. But enough serpents obeyed. When the two women went to look the next day, the tail appeared dark.

Vinata lost. She became Kadru’s slave — and when Garuda was born, he was born into slavery alongside his mother.

· · ·

The Mahabharata describes Garuda’s birth as a terrifying event. He burst from his egg with a body so bright that the gods themselves mistook him for Agni, the fire god. His wings spread across the sky. The gods trembled and went to praise him, asking him to reduce his glow, which he did.

But even with all this power, Garuda was a slave. He had to carry the serpents on his back wherever they wished to go. One day, Garuda asked them what it would take to free himself and his mother.

The serpents answered: bring us the amrita — the nectar of immortality — from heaven. Only then would they release Vinata.

Garuda went to his mother and asked for her blessing. Vinata told him to eat something first to build his strength for the journey. On Vinata’s advice, Garuda flew toward the ocean and ate an enormous number of Nishadas (a tribe of fishermen-hunters) to fill his hunger. But a Brahmana and his wife had been among the group he swallowed, and they burned his throat from inside. Garuda immediately spat them out unharmed. His father Kashyapa then directed him to eat a giant elephant and tortoise instead — who were actually two feuding brothers cursed into those forms. This gave him the strength he needed.

Garuda breaks through heaven’s defenses — no weapon could stop him

Then Garuda flew to heaven. The amrita was guarded by deadly defenses. The Adi Parva describes a spinning wheel with sharp edges, blazing like the sun, that would cut anything that tried to pass through. Beneath it, two enormous serpents with eyes like fire stood guard. Garuda shrank his body, slipped between the spokes of the wheel, blinded the two serpent-guards, and seized the amrita.

On his way out, he fought Indra himself. Indra struck Garuda with his thunderbolt — the vajra. Garuda was not hurt, but out of respect for the sage Dadhichi (from whose bones the vajra was made), he shed a single feather. The Adi Parva says that when that one feather fell, all the beings who saw it were amazed by its beauty.

It was then that Vishnu appeared before Garuda. The Mahabharata says Vishnu was pleased with Garuda’s strength and offered him a boon. Garuda asked to always stay above Vishnu — on his flagstaff — and to be immortal even without drinking the amrita. Vishnu granted both. In return, Garuda agreed to be Vishnu’s mount. This is how Garuda became Vishnu’s vahana.

Then Indra came to Garuda — no longer as an enemy, but as a friend. The Adi Parva says Indra and Garuda made a pact. Indra asked Garuda to let him take the amrita back after Vinata was freed. Garuda agreed. He also told Indra to snatch the amrita away before the serpents could actually drink it.

The serpents lick the kusha grass where the amrita sat — and their tongues are split forever

Garuda brought the amrita to the serpents and placed it before them on a bed of kusha grass. He told them: here is the amrita, as promised. Now free my mother. The serpents released Vinata.

But before the serpents could drink, Garuda told them to purify themselves first with a bath, as was proper ritual. The moment the serpents left to bathe, Indra swooped down and took the amrita back to heaven.

When the serpents returned and found the amrita gone, they were desperate. They began licking the kusha grass where the pot had sat, hoping to taste even a drop of the nectar. But kusha grass has sharp edges. As they licked it, the grass cut their tongues — splitting them down the middle.

According to the Mahabharata, this is why snakes have forked tongues to this day.

The Mystery

The serpents got exactly what they asked for. The amrita was placed right in front of them. They had won. But their own greed — the same greed that made them enslave an innocent woman and her son — was not enough to let them keep it. They lost the nectar, and the only thing they gained was a split tongue for all their kind, forever.

And Garuda, the one born into slavery, ended up higher than any of them — sitting on the flagstaff of Vishnu himself. The Mahabharata seems to be saying something very specific here: the one who fights for someone else’s freedom ends up with more power than the ones who fought only for themselves.

But wait…

Remember the serpents who refused Kadru’s order to cheat — the ones she cursed to die in a snake sacrifice? That curse came true. Generations later, a king named Janamejaya — grandson of Arjuna — held a massive sacrifice to destroy every serpent on earth. The only reason the snakes survived at all was because of a young Brahmana named Astika, who stopped the sacrifice at the last moment. That story is told in the very same Astika Parva of the Mahabharata — and it is the reason the Garuda story is told in the first place.

Characters
Garuda — the mighty bird, son of Vinata. Vinata — Garuda’s mother, enslaved by Kadru. Kadru — mother of the serpents. Kashyapa — sage, father of both Garuda and the Nagas. Aruna — Garuda’s half-formed elder brother. Indra — king of the gods. Vishnu — the preserver god. The thousand serpent sons of Kadru.
Virtue / Theme
Devotion to Mother & The Price of Greed — a son’s love conquered heaven, while the cheaters lost everything
Source
Mahabharata, Adi Parva, Astika Parva (also referred to as the Astika sub-parva and the Garuda sub-section within it), Chapters approximately 14–34 in the Ganguli translation. The bet between Kadru and Vinata (Ch 16–20), Garuda’s birth and power (Ch 23–24), the quest for amrita (Ch 25–30), the encounter with Vishnu and Indra (Ch 30–33), the forked-tongue origin (Ch 34). Verification
This episode is one of the major sub-narratives of the Adi Parva, available in K.M. Ganguli’s English translation (Adi Parva, Sections XVI–XXXIV in his numbering) and Bibek Debroy’s English translation (Volume 1). The BORI Critical Edition includes the Garuda episode in the Astika Parva section of Adi Parva. The “But Wait” teaser refers to Janamejaya’s Sarpa Satra, which is the framing narrative of the entire Astika Parva — one of the most well-attested episodes in the Mahabharata.
