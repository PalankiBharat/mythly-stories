Shiva Purana · Rudra Samhita, Parvati Khanda

The Woman Who Stood in Fire for Love — and Made Even Shiva Come to Her

Parvati, Shiva & Himavan Parvati Khanda, Chapters 20–40 Determination & Devotion
· · ·

When the god of love, Kamadeva, shot his flower arrow at Shiva, Shiva’s third eye opened and burned Kama to ash. His wife Rati would wait ages to find him again. But there was another woman who was watching that day — Parvati. For one brief moment before the fire, Shiva’s eyes had opened. He had seen her. And then he walked away, back into his meditation, as if she did not exist.

Most people would have given up. Parvati did not.

She was the daughter of Himavan, king of the mountains, and his wife Mena. Even before Kamadeva’s failed mission, Parvati had been serving Shiva at his meditation site — bringing flowers, cleaning the area, sitting nearby in quiet devotion. Her father had sent her there on the advice of the sage Narada, who told Himavan that Parvati was destined to marry Shiva. But destiny, it turned out, was not going to be handed to her. She would have to earn it.

After Shiva rejected her presence and left, Parvati made a decision. She would not chase him. She would not send another god to trick him. She would do tapas — austerities so extreme that Shiva himself would have no choice but to notice.

· · ·

She went into the forest alone. At first, her tapas was gentle — she ate only fruits and roots, slept on the ground, and meditated on Shiva. But as months passed, she made it harder. She gave up fruits and ate only leaves that had fallen to the ground. Then she gave up even the fallen leaves.

It was this act that gave her a new name. Her mother Mena, hearing that Parvati had stopped eating even leaves, cried out in anguish:

Mena “U-ma! — Oh, do not do this!”

From that cry, Parvati came to be known as Uma. And because she gave up even leaves (parna), she became known as Aparna — “she who does not eat even a leaf.”

But she did not stop there. According to the Shiva Purana, she performed the Panchagni tapas — she sat surrounded by four blazing fires with the summer sun overhead as the fifth fire. This same detail is corroborated by Kalidasa in the Kumara Sambhava (Canto 5), which draws on the same ancient tradition. In the rainy season, she sat unprotected in the open, drenched by storms. In winter, she stood in freezing water. She did this not for weeks or months, but for years.

Her father Himavan came to her, begging her to stop. He could not bear to see his daughter suffer. But Parvati would not move.

Himavan begs his daughter to stop — but Parvati will not move

Then came the test.

One day, a young brahmin arrived at her tapas site. He was charming and well-spoken. He praised Parvati’s devotion and asked her why she was doing all this. When she told him she wanted Shiva as her husband, the brahmin laughed.

He began to mock Shiva. He told Parvati that Shiva was not fit to be anyone’s husband. According to the Shiva Purana, the brahmin listed Shiva’s faults one by one: he has no home, he wanders in cremation grounds, he smears his body with ash from funeral pyres, he wears snakes around his neck, he rides a bull, he has no family or lineage that anyone knows, he keeps company with ghosts and goblins, and no one even knows where he comes from. The brahmin told Parvati she was the daughter of the mountain king — beautiful, royal, worthy of any god — and she was throwing herself away on a beggar.

Parvati listened quietly. Then her patience broke.

She told the brahmin to stop. According to the text, she defended Shiva with fierce words. She said that the brahmin was a fool who saw only the surface of things. Shiva’s ash was sacred. His cremation ground was the place where all worldly attachments end. His snakes showed his mastery over fear. His lack of possessions showed that he was above all desire. She said that only someone with no understanding would look at the lord of the universe and see a beggar.

She told the brahmin that her mind was decided and nothing in the three worlds would change it. If Shiva did not accept her, she would continue her tapas until her body turned to dust. But she would never choose another.

Then she turned to leave.

· · ·

The brahmin dropped his disguise.

It was Shiva himself. He had come in the form of a brahmin to test whether Parvati’s devotion was real — whether it would hold even when everything about him was criticized to her face.

The disguise falls away — Shiva reveals himself to Parvati

It had held.

Shiva told Parvati that he was pleased. Her tapas had moved him. Her devotion was not blind — it was fierce and knowing. She did not love an idea of him. She loved him as he truly was, cremation-ash and all. According to the Shiva Purana, Shiva then went to Himavan and formally asked for Parvati’s hand in marriage. The mountain king agreed with great joy.

The woman who had stood in fire, starved herself until she earned the name “Aparna,” and refused to break even when a god mocked her choice to her face — did not win Shiva by changing who he was. She won him by refusing to change who she was.

The Lesson

Parvati did not get what she wanted by wishing for it. She paid for it — with hunger, with pain, with years of her life, and with the willingness to be tested and mocked and still not bend. The real question this story asks is not whether you want something deeply enough. It is whether you are willing to pay what it costs — and keep paying even when no one promises it will work.

But wait…

Shiva agreed to marry Parvati. But the wedding itself was anything but ordinary. When the groom’s procession arrived at Himavan’s palace, Shiva came riding his bull Nandi, smeared in ash, wearing snakes, accompanied by ghosts, goblins, and every strange being of the cremation ground. Parvati’s mother Mena took one look at the groom and fainted. The story of Shiva and Parvati’s wedding — told in the very next chapters of the Parvati Khanda — is one of the most vivid and surprising scenes in the entire Shiva Purana.

Characters
Parvati (Uma, Aparna) — daughter of Himavan; performed extreme tapas to win Shiva. Shiva (Mahadeva) — the great ascetic; tested Parvati in the guise of a brahmin. Himavan — king of the mountains; Parvati’s father. Mena — Parvati’s mother; cried “U-ma” in anguish. Narada — the divine sage who advised Himavan that Parvati was destined for Shiva.
Virtue / Theme
Determination & Devotion — the willingness to endure extreme hardship for what you believe in, and the refusal to compromise even when tested
Source
Shiva Purana, Rudra Samhita, Parvati Khanda, Chapters approximately 20–40. Parvati’s tapas and the brahmin’s test (Shiva in disguise) are narrated across these chapters.
