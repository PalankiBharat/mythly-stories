Shiva Purana · Parvati Khanda

The Goddess Whose Husband Was Burned to Ash — and She Still Did Not Give Up

Rati & Kamadeva Chapters 13–19 & Bhagavata Purana 10.55 Devotion & Endurance
· · ·

The gods were desperate.

A demon named Tarakasura had been granted a terrible boon — only the son of Shiva could kill him. But Shiva, lost in grief after the death of his first wife Sati, had retreated into meditation so deep that nothing in the world could reach him. Years passed. Centuries. The demon grew stronger. The gods suffered.

Their only hope was Parvati, daughter of the mountain king Himavan, who loved Shiva with her whole being and had come to practice tapas beside him. But Shiva’s eyes were closed.

The gods knew they needed help. They turned to Kamadeva — the god of love, the archer who never missed — and begged him to shoot a flower arrow at Shiva’s heart. If the arrow struck, Shiva would open his eyes. He would see Parvati. And perhaps, in time, love would awaken.

Kamadeva knew the risk. He looked at his wife, Rati, the goddess of love and longing. She looked back at him without flinching.

He went.

· · ·

He drew his bow strung with bees. He nocked the flower arrow. He aimed at Shiva.

He released.

The arrow struck.

For one moment — just one moment — Shiva stirred. His eyes began to open.

And then he felt it. The intrusion. The trick. The god of love had dared to pierce his meditation.

Shiva’s third eye opened.

A beam of fire shot from it.

Kamadeva was burned to ash in an instant.

Shiva’s third eye opens — and Kamadeva is turned to ash

Rati heard.

She ran to the place where he had stood. There was nothing left. Only ash on the ground, and the faint smell of burnt flowers.

What she did next was not rage. It was not collapse. It was not silence. She wept — with the full grief of a goddess who had lost the person she loved most in the world. And when she had wept, she prayed.

She knelt before Shiva and spoke plainly: she had not sent her husband to his death. She had watched him go. She had known the risk. And she did not blame Shiva. She only asked — was there any path forward? Was love truly gone from the world?

· · ·

Shiva, who had by now opened his eyes and seen Parvati, and who carried within him the memory of his own grief for Sati, heard something in Rati’s voice that he recognized.

He made her a promise.

Shiva “Kamadeva will not remain ash. In a future age, when Krishna walks the earth, Kama will be reborn as his son — Pradyumna. Beautiful, brave, a great warrior. And you, Rati — you will find him. You will know him. You will be together again.”

Rati held that promise in her heart across ages.

In the Dvapara Yuga, when Krishna and Rukmini had a son named Pradyumna, the demon Sambara kidnapped the infant and threw him into the sea. But Pradyumna did not die — a great fish swallowed him and was caught by fishermen in Sambara’s kitchen. When the fish was cut open, a beautiful child was found inside. The kitchen-maid who found him was a woman named Mayavati.

Mayavati recognized him immediately.

Mayavati was Rati. She had waited.

Mayavati recognizes Pradyumna — the wait across ages was over

She raised him in secret, taught him, protected him. When Pradyumna grew into a young man, the divine sage Narada revealed the truth — that Pradyumna was Kamadeva reborn, that Mayavati was Rati, that the wait was over. Pradyumna slew Sambara and returned with Mayavati to Dvaraka.

The gods of love had found each other again — across death, across ages, across everything.

The Lesson

Rati did not stop at grief. She held a promise and kept going — across lifetimes, in the form of a kitchen servant, waiting for the moment the story she’d been promised would finally arrive. The question is: what promise are you holding onto? And have you given up on it too soon?

But wait…

Parvati also waited. After Kamadeva’s arrow struck Shiva for one fleeting moment before it was destroyed, Shiva did open his eyes — and he saw Parvati. But he did not fall in love easily. Parvati then undertook years of brutal tapas to win him — standing in fire in summer, sitting in snow in winter, eating only fallen leaves, then eating nothing at all. The story of Parvati’s tapas is in the same Parvati Khanda of the Shiva Purana, and it is one of the most extraordinary acts of determination in all of Hindu scripture.

Characters
Rati — goddess of love and longing; wife of Kamadeva; reborn as Mayavati. Kamadeva — god of love; burned to ash by Shiva; reborn as Pradyumna. Shiva — the great ascetic; burns Kama; grants the boon of rebirth. Parvati — for whose sake Kama was sent; Shiva’s intended. Tarakasura — the demon whose defeat required Shiva’s son. Pradyumna — son of Krishna and Rukmini; reborn Kamadeva. Sambara — demon who kidnaps Pradyumna.
Virtue / Theme
Devotion & Endurance — love that survives death, grief, and the long wait across ages
