Bhagavata Purana · Skandha 8

The Elephant Who Stopped Fighting — And That Is When God Came Running

Gajendra Moksha Chapters 2–4 Surrender & Grace
· · ·

When does God come to save you? Not when you are strong. Not when you are fighting. Not when you still believe you can handle it yourself. He comes at the exact moment you stop trying — and simply call out. This is the story of how an elephant learned that lesson in the hardest way possible.

Long ago, according to the Bhagavata Purana, there was a great mountain called Trikuta, surrounded by an ocean of milk. On this mountain lived a mighty elephant king named Gajendra. He was the leader of a large herd — strong, proud, and fearless. When he walked through the forest, even lions moved out of his way. He was surrounded by his wives and his herd at all times. He knew no weakness.

One hot day, Gajendra led his herd down the mountain to a beautiful lake. The water was cool and clear, filled with lotus flowers. Gajendra waded in happily, drinking and spraying water over himself and his family. He was enjoying himself without a care in the world.

Then, from the depths of the lake, a powerful crocodile seized his leg.

Gajendra pulled. The crocodile held on. Gajendra was the mightiest elephant alive — but the crocodile was in its own element. In the water, the crocodile grew stronger. On land, the elephant grew weaker. The Bhagavata Purana says this struggle went on for a thousand years.

At first, Gajendra’s herd tried to help. His wives stood on the shore and cried out. The other elephants pulled at him from the bank. But as time passed and nothing changed, one by one, they found they could do nothing. The water was not their element — in it they were as helpless as he was. One by one, they could only move away, powerless, not heartless.

Gajendra was alone.

· · ·

His strength was gone. His body was failing. The crocodile had not loosened its grip even slightly. And now, for the first time in his life, the great elephant king understood something he had never understood before: his own strength could not save him. His herd could not save him. Nothing in this world could save him.

And so Gajendra did something he had never done. He stopped fighting. He raised his trunk out of the water, held up a lotus flower, and called out — not to any particular god, not by any name he knew. According to the Bhagavata Purana, Gajendra offered a prayer to the supreme being who is the source and support of all that exists. He prayed to the one who is beyond name and form, the ultimate refuge of those who have no other refuge.

This prayer, known as the Gajendra Stuti, is recorded in Skandha 8, Chapter 3 of the Bhagavata Purana. In it, Gajendra does not ask for victory over the crocodile. He does not ask for his old strength back. He asks for liberation itself — freedom from the cycle of birth and death. He surrenders completely.

One by one, his entire herd found they could not help him

The moment that prayer left Gajendra’s heart, something happened far away.

The Bhagavata Purana describes that Vishnu, who was in Vaikuntha, heard the cry of surrender. He immediately rose and mounted Garuda. The text says he came swiftly. He arrived at the lake, saw the exhausted elephant with his trunk raised and the crocodile still gripping his leg, and he acted at once.

Vishnu pulled Gajendra out of the water and with his Sudarshana Chakra, killed the crocodile.

But here is what makes this story extraordinary. The crocodile was not just a crocodile. According to the Bhagavata Purana (Skandha 8, Chapter 4), the crocodile had once been a Gandharva king named Huhu. He had been cursed by the sage Devala to become a crocodile. And Gajendra was not just an elephant. He had once been a Pandya king named Indradyumna, a great devotee of Vishnu, who had been cursed by the sage Agastya to be reborn as an elephant because he had not risen to greet the sage when the sage visited him, being absorbed in worship.

Vishnu descended from Vaikuntha the moment Gajendra surrendered

When Vishnu killed the crocodile, the Gandharva Huhu was freed from his curse and regained his original form. He bowed to Vishnu and returned to his heavenly realm. And Gajendra — the elephant who had once been King Indradyumna — was granted moksha by the touch of the Lord. He was freed not just from the crocodile, but from the entire cycle of birth and death.

Vishnu did not come when Gajendra was strong. He did not come when the herd was still around. He came at the one moment when the elephant had nothing left — no strength, no pride, no hope, no one by his side — and simply called out.

That is when God runs.

The Lesson

As long as Gajendra believed he could save himself, no help came. The moment he let go of his pride, his strength, and his struggle — and simply surrendered — God came running. Most of us spend our whole lives fighting our own crocodiles, refusing to let go. The question is: what are you still gripping that you need to release — before grace can reach you?

But wait…

Gajendra’s previous birth as King Indradyumna has its own story. According to the Bhagavata Purana (Skandha 8, Chapter 4), Indradyumna was a great king of the Pandya country who had renounced his kingdom to worship Vishnu in the forest. It was during this deep meditation that sage Agastya arrived and Indradyumna, absorbed in worship, did not stand up to greet him. The angry sage cursed him to become an elephant — a creature of dull understanding. But it was this very curse that eventually led Indradyumna, as Gajendra, to the moment of total surrender that gave him moksha. Sometimes the worst thing that happens to you is the very path to your freedom.

Characters
Gajendra — the elephant king, formerly King Indradyumna of the Pandya country. Vishnu — the supreme preserver, who comes riding Garuda to rescue Gajendra. Lakshmi — implied as present in Vaikuntha. Huhu — the Gandharva king cursed to become a crocodile by sage Devala. Indradyumna — Gajendra’s previous birth, a devotee-king cursed by sage Agastya. Garuda — Vishnu’s eagle mount.
Virtue / Theme
Surrender & Grace — the moment you stop fighting and truly call out is the moment salvation arrives
Source
Bhagavata Purana, Skandha 8, Chapters 2–4. Chapter 2 describes Trikuta mountain and Gajendra’s life; Chapter 3 contains the Gajendra Stuti (the elephant’s prayer of surrender); Chapter 4 describes Vishnu’s arrival, the killing of the crocodile, and the backstories of both Gajendra (as Indradyumna cursed by Agastya) and the crocodile (as Huhu cursed by Devala).
Verification
The Gajendra Moksha episode is one of the most celebrated narratives in the Bhagavata Purana and can be verified in multiple editions: Bibek Debroy’s English translation of the Bhagavata Purana (Skandha 8), the Gita Press Gorakhpur edition, and A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada’s Srimad Bhagavatam translation and commentary (Canto 8, Chapters 2–4). The Gajendra Stuti (Chapter 3) is widely recited as a standalone prayer in Hindu worship. The backstory of Indradyumna’s curse by Agastya is in Chapter 4, verses 6–13 (approximate reference).
