The Three Arrows That Could Have Ended the Entire Mahabharata War

Barbarika & Krishna Skanda Purana / Mahabharata Tradition Sacrifice / True Heroism
· · ·

What if there was a warrior so powerful that the entire Mahabharata war — all eighteen days of it, all the millions of soldiers, all the great heroes on both sides — could have been finished in a single moment? And what if that warrior never got to fight? This is the story of Barbarika, the grandson of Bhima, and the three arrows that could have destroyed everything.

Barbarika was the son of Ghatotkacha, who was Bhima’s son from his wife Ahilavati (also called Maurvi in some versions). Even as a young man, Barbarika was a fierce devotee of the goddess. Through intense tapasya, he received three divine arrows. These were not ordinary weapons. The first arrow could mark every target that was meant to be destroyed. The second could mark every target that was meant to be saved. And the third would destroy everything that had been marked by the first arrow, leaving untouched everything marked by the second. With just these three arrows, Barbarika could finish any war.

When word spread that the great war at Kurukshetra was about to begin, Barbarika prepared to go. He had taken a vow: he would fight on the side of whichever army was losing. This seemed like a noble promise — the vow of a righteous warrior who wanted to protect the weak.

· · ·

But Krishna saw the danger in this vow. Before the war began, Krishna went to meet Barbarika, disguised as a brahmin. He asked the young warrior a simple question: which side would he fight for?

Barbarika answered honestly. He said he would fight for whichever side was losing.

Krishna then explained the problem. The Pandavas were the weaker side. So Barbarika would join them first. But the moment he joined, with his three unstoppable arrows, the Pandavas would become the stronger side instantly. The Kauravas would then be losing. So Barbarika, true to his vow, would have to switch sides. Then the Pandavas would start losing again. He would switch back. This would go on and on — and in the end, Barbarika would destroy both armies completely. Everyone would die. Only Barbarika would be left standing.

Krishna made Barbarika see that his noble-sounding vow would actually lead to the destruction of everyone on both sides.

The test of the peepal tree — every leaf bound, and one hidden under Krishna’s foot

To test Barbarika’s power, Krishna asked him to prove his claim. He pointed to a peepal tree and said: if your arrows are truly all-powerful, tie every leaf of this tree with a single arrow. Barbarika shot his arrow. It flew through the tree and began circling every leaf, binding each one. But then the arrow started circling around Krishna’s own foot. Krishna had hidden a fallen leaf under his foot. The arrow knew. It could find every target, even a hidden one. Nothing could escape it.

Krishna now understood: Barbarika was telling the truth. His three arrows really could end the war in moments. And his vow to fight for the losing side would guarantee total destruction.

· · ·

Then Krishna revealed his true form to Barbarika. He told the young warrior that for the sake of dharma, for the protection of both armies from total destruction, he needed Barbarika’s head as a gift — a daan.

Barbarika did not flinch. But he made one request before offering his life. He said that he had come all this way to witness the great war. He asked that even after his head was cut off, he be allowed to watch the entire battle.

Krishna agreed. Barbarika drew his sword, cut off his own head, and offered it to Krishna.

Krishna placed Barbarika’s head on top of a hill overlooking Kurukshetra. From that hilltop, the head of the warrior who never fought watched all eighteen days of the war.

The silent witness — the warrior who saw everything but fought nothing

When the war ended and the Pandavas won, a debate broke out among them. Each warrior claimed credit for the victory. To settle the argument, Krishna brought them to the hilltop where Barbarika’s head still watched with open eyes. They asked him: you have seen the entire war from above — who was truly responsible for the victory?

According to the tradition, Barbarika’s head answered that he had seen only two things on the battlefield: Krishna’s Sudarshana Chakra spinning everywhere, destroying the forces of adharma, and a great goddess (Kali/Mahashakti) consuming the fallen. It was not any single warrior who won. It was Krishna’s divine will that decided everything. The warriors were only instruments.

Krishna, pleased with Barbarika’s devotion and sacrifice, blessed him. He declared that in the age of Kali Yuga, Barbarika would be worshipped. Today, Barbarika is worshipped as Khatu Shyamji in Rajasthan, and his temple at Khatu is one of the most visited pilgrimage sites in the region.

The Mystery

The greatest warrior at Kurukshetra never lifted a weapon. His sacrifice was not in fighting — it was in choosing not to fight. Sometimes the most powerful thing you can do is step back. The real question Barbarika forces us to ask is this: are you brave enough to give up the thing you are best at, if using it would cause more harm than good?

But wait…

Barbarika was not the only warrior in the Mahabharata who was too powerful to be allowed to fight. His own father, Ghatotkacha, was unleashed on the Kaurava army at night specifically to force Karna into using his one-time Shakti weapon — a sacrifice Krishna planned in advance. The story of how Ghatotkacha died so that Arjuna could live is told in the Drona Parva of the Mahabharata, and it carries its own terrible lesson about the cost of war.

Characters
Barbarika — grandson of Bhima, son of Ghatotkacha, a warrior of unmatched power who possessed three divine arrows that could end any war. Krishna — the divine strategist who recognized the danger of Barbarika’s power and asked for his head as a sacrifice. Ghatotkacha — Barbarika’s father, Bhima’s half-rakshasa son. Bhima — Barbarika’s grandfather, one of the five Pandavas.
Virtue / Theme
Sacrifice / True Heroism — the courage to surrender your greatest power when using it would cause more destruction than good. The understanding that real strength is sometimes shown by stepping aside.
Source
This story of Barbarika exists primarily in the Skanda Purana tradition and in regional folk versions of the Mahabharata, particularly the Rajasthani oral tradition associated with the Khatu Shyamji temple. It is not found in the BORI Critical Edition of the Mahabharata. Some later Sanskrit manuscripts and Upapuranic texts include versions of this narrative. The core elements — three arrows, the vow to fight for the losing side, Krishna’s test, the head-donation, and the head witnessing the war — are consistent across the major regional retellings. Versions vary on whether the three arrows were granted by Brahma, Shiva, or the goddess Kamakhya (Assamese tradition, where Barbarika is called Belarsen).
Verification
Important honesty note: this story has variant sources and is not part of the Mahabharata’s critical text (BORI edition). Its primary home is in the Skanda Purana tradition and Rajasthani folk-religious literature connected to the Khatu Shyamji temple (Sikar district, Rajasthan). The Assamese tradition preserves a parallel version featuring Belarsen. Scholars such as Wendy Doniger and A.K. Ramanujan have noted that the Mahabharata exists in many regional tellings, and stories like Barbarika’s are part of this broader tradition even when absent from the critical edition. The story is widely acknowledged in Indian religious scholarship and is the basis of the living Khatu Shyamji devotional tradition. We have presented the version most commonly told across North Indian traditions, noting variant details where they exist.
