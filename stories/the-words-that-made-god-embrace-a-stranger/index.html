Valmiki Ramayana · Kishkindha Kanda

The Words That Made God Embrace a Stranger

Hanuman’s First Meeting with Rama Sargas 3–4 Excellence & Recognition
· · ·

Before the burning of Lanka, before the leap across the ocean, before any of the great deeds that would make his name immortal — Hanuman had to do something far harder. He had to walk up to two strangers and speak.

Rama and Lakshmana were wandering near Rishyamuka mountain, searching for Sita. They looked like warriors — tall, strong, carrying great bows, dressed in bark cloth. From his hiding place on the mountain peak, Sugriva, the exiled vanara king, watched them approach and felt afraid. He thought Vali, his powerful brother and enemy, had sent these two to kill him.

Sugriva turned to his most trusted minister — Hanuman, the son of Vayu, the wind god.

The task was simple: go down the mountain, find out who these men are, and learn whether they mean harm. But the task was also dangerous. If they were Vali’s agents, approaching them openly could mean death.

Hanuman did not go as himself. He changed his form and took on the appearance of a wandering ascetic — a mendicant monk, a bhikshu in simple ochre robes, staff in hand. Then he walked down the slope to meet the two strangers.

· · ·

What happened next is one of the most unusual scenes in the entire Ramayana. There is no battle. No curse. No miracle. Just one person speaking — and another person listening so closely that the words changed everything.

Hanuman approached Rama and Lakshmana and spoke. He praised them, asked who they were, and offered Sugriva’s friendship. But it was not what he said that mattered most. It was how he said it. The Valmiki Ramayana describes his speech in careful detail: every word was placed correctly, his grammar was flawless, his voice was clear and pleasant, he spoke neither too fast nor too slow, and every sentence came from the heart as well as the mind.

Rama was struck. He turned to Lakshmana and quietly shared what he had noticed about this stranger.

Rama — to Lakshmana “No one can speak this way without having mastered the Rig Veda, the Yajur Veda, and the Sama Veda. He must have studied all of grammar thoroughly, for in his long speech there is not a single error — not in his face, not in his eyes, not in his forehead, not in his brows, not in any limb. His words rise from the heart and stay in the middle register of the voice. Even an enemy with his sword already raised would have his mind calmed by such speech.”
Rama tells Lakshmana: even an enemy would lower his sword hearing such words

Think about what Rama is saying. He is not praising Hanuman for his strength, his size, or his divine powers. He is praising the way this stranger talks. He is saying: the way a person uses language tells you everything about who they are. This man has studied deeply. This man thinks clearly. This man can be trusted — because his words are perfect.

Rama then told Lakshmana that a king who had such a person as his messenger could accomplish anything in the world.

· · ·

After speaking at such length, Hanuman waited. Rama did not embrace him or rush forward. What he did was quieter — and in its way, more significant. He turned to Lakshmana and instructed him to respond warmly to this messenger. The interaction remained formal, as befits an emissary and a prince meeting for the first time. But Rama’s recognition was unmistakable and complete.

Lakshmana spoke with Hanuman in kind, welcoming words. Hanuman understood that these were not enemies. He set aside his disguise and revealed his true form — he was Hanuman, son of the wind god, minister of the exiled king Sugriva. He told them that Sugriva also had lost everything and was living in fear, and that perhaps they could help each other.

Rama was pleased. He told Lakshmana that their search for Sita might find help through Sugriva. Hanuman then carried both brothers on his shoulders up to the mountain peak where Sugriva waited. The deeper bond — the one that would become legend — was only beginning to form. But it had started here, in this careful exchange of words.

And there, on that mountain, an alliance took shape — not through a miracle or a battle, but through a conversation. Hanuman had walked down the mountain as a stranger. He was carried back up as a trusted friend.

Hanuman carries Rama and Lakshmana up Rishyamuka to meet Sugriva

This is the scene most people skip. They jump straight to the ocean leap, to the burning tail in Lanka, to the mountain of herbs carried through the sky. But before all of that, there was this: a vanara walking down a hill, disguised as a wandering mendicant, armed with nothing but his words. And those words were enough to win the recognition of God himself.

The Lesson

Hanuman’s greatest weapon was never his strength. It was the way he spoke — clearly, truthfully, with deep learning behind every word. Rama did not ask to see his power. He listened to his speech and knew everything he needed to know. How do you speak? When you open your mouth, do your words reveal depth — or do they reveal that you have nothing behind them?

But wait…

That meeting on Rishyamuka led to a fire. Rama and Sugriva made their alliance — and to seal it, they circled a sacred fire together as witnesses. But before Sugriva could help Rama find Sita, he needed something first: his own kingdom back from Vali. What followed was one of the most controversial acts Rama ever performed — shooting Vali from behind a tree. That story raises a question that has troubled scholars for thousands of years.

Characters
Hanuman — son of Vayu, minister of Sugriva, disguised as a wandering ascetic (bhikshu). Rama — prince of Ayodhya, searching for Sita. Lakshmana — Rama’s brother. Sugriva — exiled vanara king hiding on Rishyamuka mountain.
Virtue / Theme
Excellence & Recognition — mastery of speech as the highest form of intelligence, and how true greatness is recognized instantly
Source
Valmiki Ramayana, Kishkindha Kanda, Sarga 3 (Hanuman’s approach and speech to Rama and Lakshmana; Rama’s praise of Hanuman’s eloquence, approximately verses 28–32) and Sarga 4 (Hanuman reveals his identity; carries them to Sugriva). Rama’s specific praise of Hanuman’s mastery of the three Vedas and grammar is in Sarga 3.
Verification
This episode is found in the Valmiki Ramayana, Kishkindha Kanda, Sargas 3–4. It can be verified in the Baroda Critical Edition of the Valmiki Ramayana, as well as in English translations by Hari Prasad Shastri, Robert Goldman (Princeton/Clay Sanskrit Library), and the Gita Press edition. Rama’s specific praise of Hanuman’s speech — including the observation about the Rig Veda, Yajur Veda, Sama Veda, and grammar — is among the most cited passages from Kishkindha Kanda in Sanskrit literary scholarship.
