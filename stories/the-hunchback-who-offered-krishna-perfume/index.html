Bhagavata Purana · Skandha 10

The Hunchbacked Woman Who Gave Krishna Perfume — and Had Her Entire Body Transformed

Kubja & Krishna in Mathura Chapters 42 & 48 Devotion & Grace
· · ·

On the day Krishna and Balarama walked into Mathura to face Kamsa, they passed through streets full of curious eyes.

People crowded the road. Some threw flowers. Some stared in silence. Among the crowd was a woman named Kubja — called Trivakra, the triple-bent one, because her spine curved at three places and her body was folded into itself. She worked as a keeper of sandalwood paste in Kamsa’s service, carrying fragrant unguents for the king.

She was not beautiful. She was not powerful. She had no boon to offer a god.

But she had sandalwood paste. And she had a heart that recognized something in the two young men walking through her city.

· · ·

When Krishna approached her and asked for some of the paste, she did not hesitate. She did not wonder whether her offering was worthy. She did not wait for a better moment. She simply gave — holding out the fragrant paste with both hands, freely, without expecting anything in return.

Krishna smiled.

He pressed his toes gently on her feet to steady her. Then he placed his fingers under her chin and slowly, with a single upward movement, lifted.

Something shifted.

The triple bend straightened. The curved spine lengthened. The folded body opened. Kubja stood up — fully upright, tall, beautiful, transformed — for the first time in her life.

Krishna lifts — and the triple bend straightens for the first time

She looked at her own hands. She looked at Krishna. She was overwhelmed.

She did not want him to leave.

Krishna told her gently that he would come back. He had a task to finish first.

He walked on toward Kamsa.

· · ·

Later, after the wrestling match, after Kamsa fell, after the city had exhaled — Krishna kept his word. He came to Kubja’s house. He sat with her. In a city full of noblemen and warriors and devotees all competing for a moment of his attention, he chose to go to the house of the woman who had given him sandalwood paste on the road.

The Bhagavata Purana does not explain this at great length. It simply records it as fact: Krishna went to Kubja’s house and spent time with her.

In a city competing for his attention, Krishna chose to go to her house

That is perhaps the whole point.

She gave what she had, without calculating its worth. She did not ask whether it was enough. And it was enough — not because the gift was magnificent, but because the giving was whole.

The Lesson

Kubja gave what she had, not what she wished she had. She did not wait to be rich, or beautiful, or important. She just gave. And she was transformed — not because the gift was grand, but because it was given fully. The question for us is: what are you holding back because you think it is not good enough to offer?

But wait…

Just moments before meeting Kubja, on the same road into Mathura, Krishna and Balarama passed a washerman (rajaka) carrying fine clothes. Krishna asked for some cloth. The washerman refused — mockingly, with contempt. He was killed by Krishna on the spot. Two people. The same request. One gave freely and was lifted up. The other refused and was destroyed. That contrast — the gatekeeper and the giver, in the same chapter, within minutes of each other — is one of the most quietly devastating teachings in the Bhagavata Purana.

Characters
Kubja (also Trivakra) — hunchbacked keeper of sandalwood paste in Kamsa’s service. Krishna — on his way into Mathura to face Kamsa. Balarama — Krishna’s elder brother, present with him.
Virtue / Theme
Devotion & Grace — a wholehearted offering, however small, is enough
Source
Bhagavata Purana, Skandha 10, Chapter 42 (Krishna’s meeting with Kubja; the washerman/rajaka who refused is in Chapter 41); Chapter 48, verses approximately 1–11 (Krishna’s visit to her home).
