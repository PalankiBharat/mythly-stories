Devi Mahatmya · Chapter 11

The Goddess Who Fed the Starving World With Her Own Tears

Shakambhari & Shatakshi Verses 46–50 Compassion as Power
· · ·

There was a time when the world forgot the gods.

A demon named Durgama had stolen the four Vedas from the heavens. Without the Vedas, the priests could not perform rituals. Without rituals, the gods grew weak. Without the rains the gods controlled, the earth dried up. A hundred years passed without a single drop of rain. Rivers shrank to dust. Crops died. Animals perished. People starved by the millions.

The gods and sages, helpless and desperate, came together and called out to the Great Goddess — the Devi — with prayers and tears.

She heard them.

· · ·

The Devi appeared in a form the world had never seen. She had a hundred eyes — shining, compassionate, seeing every suffering being on earth at once. And when she saw the scale of the hunger and pain, she wept.

But her tears were not ordinary tears. From her hundred eyes, rivers of water flowed — clean, life-giving, flooding the cracked earth. For nine continuous nights she wept, and the earth drank. This expanded account of her weeping comes from the Devi Bhagavata Purana, where she is also known as Shatakshi — the hundred-eyed one — before taking her Shakambhari form.

Shatakshi weeps — and from her hundred eyes, rivers are born

Then something extraordinary happened. From her own body, the Devi produced vegetables, fruits, and grains — enough to feed the entire world. She became the earth’s provider, carrying sustenance in her own form. This is why she was given the name Shakambhari — “She who bears and nourishes with vegetables.”

· · ·

When every creature had been fed, she turned to the demon.

She slew Durgama and reclaimed the four Vedas. The rituals could resume. The rains returned. The world lived.

Shakambhari slays Durgama — the Vedas reclaimed, the rains returned

But before she departed, the Devi made a promise to the sages who had prayed to her:

Shakambhari Devi “Whenever the world is afflicted by drought, by famine, by despair — I will appear again as Shakambhari and nourish all beings from my own body.”
The Lesson

The Devi didn’t just defeat the demon. She fed the world first. Strength without compassion is just violence. The question for us is: when was the last time you noticed someone going hungry — in any sense of the word — and actually did something about it?

But wait…

In the very same passage of the Devi Mahatmya, the goddess makes another promise — that she will appear in the future as Bhramari Devi, the Goddess of Bees, when a demon named Aruna threatens the world. In that form, she will fight not with weapons but with millions of bees who emerge from her own body. That story has never been made into a major film or TV series. Almost nobody knows it. And it is entirely from scripture.

Characters
Shakambhari Devi — the Goddess, the nourisher; also known as Shatakshi (hundred-eyed). Durgama — the demon who stole the four Vedas. The gods and sages — who prayed to the Devi in desperation.
Virtue / Theme
Compassion as Power — True strength is not only the power to fight; it is the willingness to nourish and care for others
Source
Devi Mahatmya, Chapter 11 (= Markandeya Purana, Chapter 91), verses approximately 46–50. Expanded account in Devi Bhagavata Purana, Skandha 7, Chapter 28.
