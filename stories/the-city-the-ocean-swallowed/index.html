Mahabharata · Mausala Parva & Bhagavata Purana · Skandha 11

The City the Ocean Swallowed — What Really Happened to Krishna’s Dwaraka

The End of the Yadavas Mausala Parva, Chapters 1–7 Fate & Inevitability
· · ·

How does a city built by a god disappear from the face of the earth? How does an entire clan — blessed by Krishna himself — destroy itself in a single afternoon? And how does the same piece of iron that was ground to dust and thrown into the sea come back, years later, as the arrow that kills Krishna? This is not a fable. According to the Mahabharata, this is exactly what happened.

It began with a joke. A very stupid joke.

Years after the great war at Kurukshetra, some young men of the Yadava clan — Krishna’s own family — decided to play a prank on a group of visiting sages. They dressed up Samba, Krishna’s son, as a pregnant woman and brought him before the rishis. They asked, with mocking laughter, what kind of child this “woman” would give birth to.

The sages were not amused. They were Vishvamitra, Kanva, Narada, and others — men whose words carried the weight of fate itself. They saw through the disguise instantly.

The Sages “This one will give birth to an iron pestle — and it will destroy the entire Yadava race.”

The next day, an iron pestle came out of Samba’s body. The Yadava elders were terrified. King Ugrasena ordered the pestle to be ground into fine powder and thrown into the sea near the coast of Prabhasa. They thought that would end it. One small piece remained that could not be ground down, so it too was thrown into the water.

But the iron powder did not disappear. Over time, it settled along the shoreline. And from that iron-laced sand, a kind of sharp, tough reed — called eraka grass — grew along the beaches of Prabhasa.

The leftover piece of iron was swallowed by a fish. A fisherman later caught the fish and found the iron lump inside. It was taken by a hunter named Jara, who fixed it as the tip of one of his arrows.

· · ·

Krishna knew. The Bhagavata Purana makes it clear — Krishna knew exactly what was coming. He did not stop it. According to the text, the curse was part of his own plan to end the Yadava dynasty before he left the world, because if they continued without him, their power would become a danger to the earth.

Strange signs began appearing in Dwaraka. Rats multiplied in the city. The sacred fires in temples burned in strange directions. The day and the night seemed confused. The Mausala Parva lists these omens in detail. Krishna told the Yadavas to go on a pilgrimage to Prabhasa, on the seacoast.

There, on the beach, they began to drink. The Mahabharata says they drank heavily — wine made from rice. And as they drank, old arguments surfaced. Satyaki and Kritavarma began to quarrel about what each had done during the Kurukshetra war. Satyaki mocked Kritavarma for the night massacre of the sleeping Pandava camp. Kritavarma threw back Satyaki’s killing of unarmed warriors.

Words turned to blows. Satyaki struck Kritavarma’s head. And then, all at once, the entire Yadava army turned on each other.

The Yadavas destroy each other at Prabhasa — with reeds born from the same iron they threw into the sea

When they ran out of weapons, they grabbed the reeds growing along the shore — the eraka grass that had grown from the iron powder. In their hands, these reeds became hard as iron, as deadly as maces. The Mausala Parva says every reed they touched became a weapon of destruction. Brother killed brother. Father killed son.

Krishna’s own son Pradyumna was killed. His grandson Aniruddha fell. Satyaki, Kritavarma, Samba — all dead. Almost the entire Yadava clan wiped itself out on that one beach, in one afternoon.

Krishna himself picked up the reeds and fought when some attacked him. The Mahabharata says he killed many in that battle. Balarama, his brother, did not fight. He walked to the edge of the sea, sat down in meditation, and left his body — the Mausala Parva says a great white serpent (Shesha) was seen leaving from his mouth, going toward the ocean.

· · ·

After the slaughter, Krishna walked alone into the forest near Prabhasa. He sat beneath a tree, one foot resting over his other knee, the sole exposed. His foot was dark. From a distance, it looked like the ear of a deer.

The hunter Jara saw it through the trees. He released his arrow — the arrow tipped with the last piece of iron from the pestle, the one piece that could not be ground down, the one that had been swallowed by a fish and recovered.

The arrow struck Krishna’s foot.

When Jara ran forward and realized what he had done, he fell at Krishna’s feet in horror. The Bhagavata Purana (Skandha 11, Chapter 30) records that Krishna comforted him and told him not to grieve, that this was already decided.

The arrow that could not be destroyed found the one place it was meant to reach

Krishna departed from the world.

When Arjuna arrived at Dwaraka — sent by the Pandavas after they received the news — he found a city already beginning to die. The Mausala Parva says that as Arjuna led the remaining women and children out of the city, the ocean began to swallow Dwaraka behind them, street by street. Whatever ground Arjuna’s people left behind, the sea covered immediately.

The golden city of Krishna — the city said to have been built by Vishwakarma himself on land reclaimed from the sea — returned to the sea.

The Mystery

The iron could not be escaped. Ground to powder, it became reeds. Thrown into the sea, it came back in a fish. Fixed to an arrow, it found the one target that mattered. The entire arc — from a prank played on sages to the flooding of a city — was, according to the scriptures, set in motion by a single act of disrespect. And Krishna, who could have stopped any of it, chose not to.

In 2001, the National Institute of Ocean Technology (NIOT) and the Archaeological Survey of India conducted underwater surveys off the coast of modern Dwarka in Gujarat. They found submerged stone structures, walls, and what appear to be ruins of an ancient settlement beneath the Arabian Sea. Dating and identification remain debated among archaeologists, and no confirmed link to the mythological city has been established. But researchers have noted it as a remarkable coincidence: suggestive evidence, not proof — structures beneath the water, in the general region where the Mahabharata placed them.

But wait…

When Arjuna was leading the survivors out of Dwaraka, bandits attacked the caravan on the road. Arjuna — the greatest archer who ever lived, the wielder of the Gandiva — tried to fight them off. And he could not. His arrows had lost their power. His arms had lost their strength. The Gandiva, which had never failed him, felt like a dead weight. The Mausala Parva records this moment: the instant Krishna left the world, Arjuna’s divine power left him too. That story — of the greatest warrior discovering he was nothing without his friend — is one of the most heartbreaking passages in the entire Mahabharata.

Characters
Krishna — the lord of Dwaraka. Samba — Krishna’s son, dressed as a woman in the prank. Balarama — Krishna’s elder brother. Satyaki and Kritavarma — Yadava warriors. Jara — the hunter. Arjuna — who comes to evacuate the city. The sages Vishvamitra, Kanva, Narada, and others.
Virtue / Theme
Fate & Inevitability — some consequences cannot be outrun, only accepted
