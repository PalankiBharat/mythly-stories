Bhagavata Purana · Skandha 10, Chapter 88

The Demon Who Got the Most Dangerous Boon — Then Used It on Himself

Vrikasura & Lord Shiva Verses 14–37 Pride & Self-Destruction
· · ·

There was once a demon named Vrika, the son of Shakuni. He was not the strongest demon. He was not the wisest. But he was hungry for power — and that hunger made him dangerous.

One day, Vrika met the wandering sage Narada on the road and asked him a simple question: which god is the easiest to please?

Narada, who always spoke the truth, told him plainly that Lord Shiva was the quickest to be satisfied. Shiva did not need long rituals or years of prayer. He saw even the smallest good quality in a devotee and was moved by it. But Narada also warned him — Shiva had given boons to demons like Ravana and Bana before, and those boons had led them to ruin.

Vrika did not care about the warning. He only heard what he wanted to hear.

· · ·

He went straight to Kedarnath, high in the Himalayas, and began his worship. But this was no ordinary prayer. Vrika lit a sacred fire and, day after day, cut pieces of flesh from his own body and offered them into the flames. For six days, he burned his own flesh as an offering to Shiva.

On the seventh day, Shiva still had not appeared. So Vrika decided to end it. He soaked his hair with water, raised a hatchet, and prepared to cut off his own head.

At that moment, Shiva rose from the sacrificial fire. He caught Vrika’s arms and stopped him. The Lord healed the demon’s wounded body with the touch of his hands and told Vrika to ask for any boon he wished.

Vrika asked for the most terrible thing he could think of.

Vrikasura “May death come to whoever’s head I touch with my hand.”

Shiva granted the boon. The Bhagavata Purana says he did so with an ironic smile — like someone giving milk to a poisonous snake.

Shiva grants the boon — like giving milk to a poisonous snake

And then Vrika did the unthinkable.

The moment he received the power, he turned around and tried to place his hand on Shiva’s own head. The god who had just healed his body, who had just given him everything he asked for — Vrika wanted to kill him. Some say he wanted to take Shiva’s wife, Parvati. Whatever the reason, his arrogance was complete. He believed no one — not even the god who blessed him — was above his power now.

Shiva ran.

The scripture says the Lord fled to the ends of the earth, across the sky, to the very corners of the universe. And Vrika chased him, drunk on his new power, his hand raised and ready.

· · ·

Finally, Lord Vishnu saw what was happening. He appeared before Vrika — not in his grand four-armed form, but disguised as a young brahmachari student. He wore a simple belt, a deerskin, carried a wooden rod and prayer beads. He looked humble and harmless.

The disguised Vishnu greeted Vrika warmly and asked why he looked so tired. Vrika, seeing no threat in this simple student, told him the whole story — the boon, the chase, all of it.

Vishnu listened. Then, very calmly, he planted a seed of doubt.

Vishnu (disguised as a brahmachari) “Shiva is the lord of ghosts and spirits. Daksha once cursed him. If I were you, I would not trust his words. Why don’t you test the boon on yourself — touch your own head — and see if it even works?”

It was so simple. So quiet. No battle. No great weapon. Just a few clever words aimed at the one place Vrika had no armor — his pride.

Because a man drunk on power cannot imagine being wrong. And a man who cannot imagine being wrong will believe anything that flatters his doubt in others.

The hand that was meant for Shiva found its true target

Vrika placed his hand on his own head.

His skull shattered instantly, as if struck by a thunderbolt. He fell dead on the spot.

The scripture says that the gods in heaven rained down flowers and cried out in celebration. The threat was over. And it had ended not with a war, but with the demon’s own hand.

The Lesson

Vrika’s power did not destroy him. His arrogance did. The moment he believed he was above the very god who blessed him, he lost the ability to think clearly — and a few simple words were enough to end him. Pride does not make you strong. It makes you easy to fool. The question is: what power in your own life has made you stop listening to reason?

But wait…

Narada told Vrika that Shiva was the easiest god to please. But the same chapter of the Bhagavata Purana asks a deeper question: why do Shiva’s devotees often end up poor and struggling, while Vishnu’s devotees gain lasting peace? The answer Shiva himself gives — about why he sometimes takes away wealth from those he loves — is one of the most surprising teachings in all the Puranas.

Characters
Vrikasura (also called Vrika) — a demon, son of Shakuni. Narada — the wandering sage. Lord Shiva — who grants the boon. Lord Vishnu — who appears disguised as a brahmachari student.
Virtue / Theme
Pride & Self-Destruction — unchecked arrogance becomes the weapon that destroys you
Source
Bhagavata Purana (Srimad Bhagavatam), Skandha 10, Chapter 88 (“Lord Shiva Saved from Vrikasura”), verses 14–37. Verification
This episode is found in the Bhagavata Purana, Skandha 10, Chapter 88. Available in multiple published translations including the Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada edition (BBT), Bibek Debroy’s translation, and the Gita Press Gorakhpur edition. The “But Wait” teaser refers to verses 8–13 of the same chapter, where the Bhagavata discusses why Shiva’s material boons can lead devotees to ruin. Note: the popular folk version where Vishnu appears as Mohini (the enchantress) is not found in this chapter of the Bhagavata Purana. In the original text, Vishnu appears as a brahmachari student.
