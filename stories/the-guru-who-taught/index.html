Vishnu Purana · Book 2

The Guru Who Taught His Student by Climbing on His Back

Ribhu & Nidagha Chapters 15–16 Non-Duality (Advaita)
· · ·

Long ago, Brahma had a son named Ribhu. Ribhu was wise from birth — he understood the deepest truth of existence: that all of reality is one, and there is no real difference between one being and another. Everything is Vasudeva — the Supreme Spirit.

Ribhu had a devoted student named Nidagha, the son of sage Pulastya. They lived together near the city of Viranagara, on the banks of the river Devika. Ribhu taught Nidagha everything about the oneness of all existence. When the teaching was complete, Ribhu believed his student had understood.

But Nidagha went back to his city life. He became busy with rituals, ceremonies, and the outward forms of religion. He still saw the world as full of separate things — this is good food, that is bad food; this person is high, that person is low. The knowledge of oneness had not truly sunk in.

A thousand divine years passed. Ribhu decided to visit his old student. He arrived at Nidagha's house in disguise, just as Nidagha was finishing a ritual. Nidagha did not recognize him and offered him a meal.

According to the Vishnu Purana, when Ribhu asked what food was available, Nidagha listed various types — cakes, rice, barley, pulse — and asked his guest which he preferred. Ribhu then asked for sweeter, finer food. After the meal, Ribhu asked:

Ribhu (Disguised) “Are you satisfied? Where does satisfaction come from — from the food, or from the one who eats it?”

Then he taught Nidagha that there is no real difference between sweet food and plain food — they are all made of the same earth, the same atoms. The person who sees differences everywhere is still trapped in illusion. Nidagha was shaken. He realized this stranger was no ordinary man and begged him to reveal himself. Ribhu told him who he was, taught him to see all things as one undivided reality, and departed.

Ribhu in disguise teaches Nidagha that all food — sweet or plain — is made of the same earth
· · ·

Another thousand divine years passed. Ribhu came again. This time, he found Nidagha standing outside the city, watching a grand royal procession from a distance. He was keeping away from the crowd.

Ribhu, again in disguise as a simple villager, approached and asked: What is happening here?

Nidagha said: The king is going in a procession.

Ribhu asked: Which one is the king?

Nidagha pointed: The one sitting on the elephant.

Ribhu asked: You have shown me two together. But which one is the king and which is the elephant?

Nidagha, getting annoyed, said: The one above is the king. The one below is the elephant. How can you not know this?

Ribhu asked innocently: What does “above” mean? What does “below” mean?

Nidagha, frustrated, jumped onto Ribhu's back and said: Look! I am above, like the king. You are below, like the elephant. Now do you understand?

Ribhu “You are like the king and I am like the elephant. But tell me — which one is ‘you’ and which one is ‘me’?”

The moment Nidagha heard those words, everything became clear. He jumped down, fell at his teacher's feet, and said: Only my guru Ribhu could ask such a question. No one else has a mind so clean and so sharp.

“Which one is ‘you’ and which one is ‘me’?” — the question that shattered all illusion

According to the Vishnu Purana, Ribhu then told Nidagha: The essence of the highest truth is that there is no duality in anything. Know this whole universe to be the one undivided nature of the Supreme Spirit, called Vasudeva.

Then Ribhu departed. And Nidagha, finally understanding, saw the same divine Self in all beings without any difference. He merged into the Supreme Brahman and attained liberation.

The Lesson

We spend our lives sorting the world into boxes — rich and poor, above and below, yours and mine, good and bad. Nidagha knew the philosophy of oneness. He could recite it. But he still lived in duality — judging food, avoiding crowds, separating king from elephant. It took one question from his guru to shatter all those walls: “Which one is you and which one is me?” The question is — do you only understand oneness in theory, or do you actually live it?

But wait…

This story was not told for its own sake. In the Vishnu Purana, it is told by Bharata — the great king who gave up his throne, was reborn as a deer due to attachment, and was reborn again as the seemingly dull Jadabharata. It was Jadabharata who told this story to the king of Souvira, to wake him up from the same illusion Nidagha suffered from. The tale of how a king became a deer and then a silent madman — that is one of the most remarkable stories in the Vishnu Purana's second book.

Characters
Ribhu (son of Brahma, the guru), Nidagha (son of Pulastya, the student), Bharata (the narrator within the Purana, also known as Jadabharata)
Virtue / Theme
Non-Duality (Advaita), True Knowledge vs. Ritual Knowledge, The Guru–Disciple Bond
Source
Vishnu Purana, Book 2 (Amsha 2), Chapters 15–16. Chapter 15 covers Ribhu's first visit and the food dialogue. Chapter 16 covers the second visit with the royal procession and the “above and below” teaching. The story is framed as a narration by Bharata (Jadabharata) to the king of Souvira.
