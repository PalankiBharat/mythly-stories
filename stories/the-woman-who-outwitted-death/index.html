Mahabharata · Vana Parva · Savitri Upakhyana

The Woman Who Followed Death — and Out-Argued Him Five Times

Savitri & Satyavan Chapters 293–299 (Ganguli) Intelligence & Devotion
· · ·

Everyone told her not to choose him. The sage Narada himself warned her. Satyavan, the prince she loved, was destined to die exactly one year from that day. Choose another husband, they said. Any other man. But Savitri, the princess of Madra, had already made her choice.

Savitri “Whether his life is long or short, whether he has virtues or not — I have chosen my husband once. I will not choose a second time.”

Her father, King Ashvapati, heard the firmness in her voice and gave his blessing. Savitri married Satyavan and went to live with him and his blind, exiled father Dyumatsena in a simple forest ashram. She set aside her royal clothes, wore bark garments, and served her in-laws with care.

She told no one about the prophecy. She carried it alone.

As the final day drew near, Savitri began a three-day fast — a vow called triratra. She ate nothing. She stood in prayer. Her father-in-law, worried, asked her to break her fast. She politely refused, saying she had taken a vow and would eat only after it was complete.

· · ·

On the morning of the last day, Satyavan picked up his axe and went into the forest to gather wood. Savitri asked to come with him. He smiled and agreed.

Deep in the forest, while chopping a tree, Satyavan suddenly felt a terrible pain in his head. He stumbled to Savitri, said his head felt like it was pierced with needles, and lay down with his head in her lap.

Then she saw him.

Yama — the god of death himself. Not a messenger, not an assistant. Yama had come personally, dark-skinned, wearing red garments, with a noose in his hand. He drew out from Satyavan’s body a tiny being, the size of a thumb — the soul — and bound it in his noose. Satyavan’s body went still.

Yama draws Satyavan’s soul — and Savitri watches, unafraid

Yama turned south and began to walk away. And Savitri stood up and followed him.

Yama told her to go back. He said her duties to her husband’s body were done, and she should return. But Savitri kept walking.

She did not beg. She did not weep. She began to speak — about dharma, about the duty of the righteous, about what it means to walk in the company of the good. Her words were so wise, so well-spoken, that Yama was pleased.

Yama “Your words are full of wisdom and bring comfort. Ask me any boon — except the life of Satyavan.”

Savitri asked for the first boon: that her blind father-in-law Dyumatsena have his eyesight restored. Yama granted it and told her to turn back. She kept walking.

She spoke again — about how the good never walk without purpose, about how meeting a righteous soul is never without fruit. Again Yama was moved by her wisdom.

He offered a second boon — anything except Satyavan’s life. Savitri asked that Dyumatsena have his lost kingdom restored. Granted. Yama told her to turn back. She kept walking.

A third time she spoke — about truth, about how the conduct of the good is the highest dharma in all the worlds. Yama offered a third boon. Again, anything except Satyavan’s life.

Savitri asked for a hundred sons for her own father, King Ashvapati. Yama granted it and urged her again to go back. She kept walking.

· · ·

A fourth time she spoke — about the noble, about how those who devote themselves to dharma are trusted even by the gods. Yama was deeply impressed. He offered a fourth boon. Again, anything except Satyavan’s life.

Savitri asked for a hundred sons born through Satyavan — her own sons, from her own husband. Yama smiled and granted it, bidding her turn back. She kept walking.

And now Yama, more and more moved by this extraordinary woman, offered a fifth boon.

He spoke the words — and this time, in his admiration, he did not add the restriction.

Savitri heard it. She noticed immediately.

Savitri “Then grant me this: let Satyavan live, so that I may have those hundred sons you have promised me.”
The fifth boon — and the moment Savitri’s sharp mind caught what Yama had left unsaid

Yama stopped. He had offered the boon without condition. He had given his word. And Savitri — who had been listening with complete attention through every exchange — had caught the one unguarded moment in the god of death’s speech.

Yama was bound by his own word. He could not take back what he had freely offered. The god of dharma recognised that a mortal woman, armed with nothing but patience, wisdom, and a mind that missed nothing, had held him to his own standard. He released Satyavan’s soul from his noose.

Savitri walked back through the forest and found Satyavan’s body under the tree. As she reached him, he opened his eyes, as if waking from sleep.

Satyavan “I have slept for a long time. Why did you not wake me?”

Savitri held him and said nothing about what she had done. They walked home together through the dark forest. At the ashram, Dyumatsena could see again. In time, his kingdom was restored. Ashvapati received his hundred sons. And Savitri — the woman who chose with full knowledge, prepared without complaint, and caught the one word death forgot to say — lived with Satyavan in happiness.

The Lesson

Savitri did not deny the truth. She did not run from it. She looked at the worst possible outcome, chose anyway, and then prepared. She did not fight death with anger or tears — she fought him with intelligence and dharma. She listened so completely that she heard the one thing he forgot to say. The question is: when you know something hard is coming, do you avoid it? Or do you walk straight toward it, paying attention to every word?

But wait…

Savitri’s father, King Ashvapati, had his own extraordinary story. He performed eighteen years of daily worship to the goddess Savitri (a form of Saraswati) just to get a child. It was this goddess who appeared and blessed him with a daughter — whom he named Savitri after her. That story of a father’s eighteen-year prayer is told in the same Savitri Upakhyana, in the chapters just before this one.

Characters
Savitri — princess of Madra, wife of Satyavan, whose intelligence defeated death. Satyavan — exiled prince, son of Dyumatsena, Savitri’s chosen husband. Yama — the god of death and dharma, who came personally to take Satyavan’s soul. Dyumatsena — Satyavan’s blind, exiled father. Narada — the sage who warned Savitri of Satyavan’s fate. Ashvapati — king of Madra, Savitri’s father.
Virtue / Theme
Intelligence & Devotion — choosing with full knowledge, preparing without complaint, and fighting the impossible with wisdom rather than force
Source
Mahabharata, Vana Parva, Savitri Upakhyana, Chapters approximately 293–299 (Ganguli edition). Also known as the Pativrata Mahatmya. The story is narrated by the sage Markandeya to Yudhishthira. BORI Critical Edition: Aranyaka Parva, chapters 277–283 (approximate).
Verification
The Mahabharata describes five boons, not three. Boon 1: Dyumatsena’s eyesight restored. Boon 2: Dyumatsena’s kingdom restored. Boon 3: A hundred sons for Ashvapati (Savitri’s own father). Boon 4: A hundred sons born through Satyavan. Boon 5: Yama, deeply moved by Savitri’s wisdom, offered this boon without adding the customary restriction “except Satyavan’s life.” Savitri immediately noticed the omission and asked directly for Satyavan’s life. Yama, bound by his own freely given word, conceded. Available in K.M. Ganguli’s English translation of Vana Parva (publicly available) and Bibek Debroy’s translation (Volume 3).
